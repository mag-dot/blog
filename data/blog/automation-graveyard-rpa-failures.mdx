# The Automation Graveyard: Post-Mortem of 5 Real RPA Failures (And a Checklist So You Don't Join Them)

Here's a number that should make every operations leader uncomfortable: **up to 50% of initial RPA projects fail outright**, according to Ernst & Young.

And those are just the projects that fail loudly. The quiet failures are worse. The bots that technically "work" but cost more to maintain than the humans they replaced. The pilot that succeeded but never scaled beyond 10 bots. The automation program that consumed 18 months and $1.6M only to become a full-time maintenance job.

We analysed dozens of post-mortems, industry reports, and case studies to pattern-match the five failure modes that keep appearing in the wreckage. If any of these sound familiar, your program might already be in the graveyard — you just haven't read the headstone yet.

---

## Failure #1: Automating the Mess

**The pattern:** A company automates a broken process and expects the bot to fix what humans couldn't.

**What happened:** A European telecom provider deployed RPA across its finance unit to automate invoice processing, order entries, and bank reconciliations. The bots were built to mirror exactly how human operators navigated their disconnected ERP systems — clicking through the same fragmented workflows, copying data between the same siloed applications.

The result? The bots replicated every inefficiency at machine speed. When exceptions arose (and they did, constantly), there was no process logic to handle them because no one had documented the actual process. Researchers studying the implementation found that process documentation was eventually abandoned entirely because "it was much easier for a developer to just go into the process and step through it" — a red flag that the underlying workflows were never properly understood in the first place.

**The autopsy:** RPA doesn't fix processes. It fossilises them. If your workflow requires seven systems, three workarounds, and a spreadsheet that only one person on the team understands — the bot will faithfully reproduce that chaos. Except now you can't fix it without also rewriting the bot.

**The lesson:** Before you automate anything, ask: "If I described this process to a new hire in writing, would they be able to follow it without asking someone for help?" If not, you're not ready for RPA. You're ready for process redesign.

> **EY data:** 30–50% of initial RPA projects fail. The most common root cause isn't technology — it's automating processes that were never well-defined.

## Failure #2: The Department of 50 Fragile Bots

**The pattern:** Each team builds their own bots, creating a sprawling estate of uncoordinated automation with no governance.

**What happened:** A multinational bank's innovation team rolled out an RPA program with decentralised ownership — each department got their own bots, their own developers, and their own priorities. Within 18 months they had roughly 50 bots across finance, compliance, and operations.

The problem surfaced when the firm's ERP system pushed a quarterly update. Twelve bots broke simultaneously. But because each bot had been built by a different team with different standards (and in some cases, no documentation at all), the remediation took weeks rather than hours. Some developers had left the company. Others had built workarounds so idiosyncratic that no one else could debug them.

An internal review found that the firm's Centre of Excellence — originally set up to govern the program — had become a bottleneck that teams routinely circumvented. Bots were being deployed directly to production without quality assurance review because "we couldn't wait for them to have time to review it," as one project lead put it.

**The autopsy:** Shadow automation is the enterprise equivalent of shadow IT, but worse — because bots interact with production systems, handle financial data, and process customer information. When they break, they don't just stop working. They produce wrong outputs that look right.

**The lesson:** Governance isn't bureaucracy; it's insurance. Every bot should have an owner, documentation, and a defined maintenance schedule. If your automation estate can't answer "who owns this bot and when was it last tested?" for every single deployment — you have a graveyard in waiting.

> **Industry data:** More than 50% of RPA projects never grow beyond 10 bots. Over 70% plateau at fewer than 50 bots (Forrester). The scaling wall isn't technical — it's organisational.

## Failure #3: The Set-It-and-Forget-It Myth

**The pattern:** Leadership treats bots as "done" after deployment, budgets zero for maintenance, and is shocked when everything breaks.

**What happened:** A logistics company deployed bots to automate sales order creation, invoice processing, and shipment tracking. The initial deployment delivered exactly what was promised — faster turnaround, fewer errors, and real cost savings. Management declared victory.

Then the underlying systems started updating. A supplier portal redesigned its interface. SAP pushed a patch that shifted field locations. A web application changed its login flow. Each change broke one or more bots. But the maintenance budget had been zero from the start because RPA vendors had sold the technology as "set and forget."

HfS Research estimates that software licensing represents only **25–30% of RPA's total cost of ownership**. The remaining **70–75%** goes to implementation, maintenance, emergency fixes, and developer premiums. For a typical 50-bot enterprise deployment, that translates to roughly **$680,000 in maintenance costs over three years** — money that was never in the original business case.

**The autopsy:** RPA bots are not software products. They're integrations duct-taped to the surfaces of other software products. When those surfaces change — and they will, because modern enterprise software updates quarterly at minimum — the bots break. Period.

One experienced RPA manager described the maintenance reality bluntly: "I think one of the things that always surprised me was the significant amount of work required to keep the robots running."

**The lesson:** Budget for maintenance on day one, or don't start. A healthy RPA program allocates **at least 20–30% of implementation cost annually** for ongoing maintenance, bot retraining, and emergency remediation. If your business case only works with zero maintenance cost, your business case is fiction.

> **HfS Research:** Licensing is only 25–30% of RPA TCO. The rest — 70–75% — is services, maintenance, and ongoing support.

## Failure #4: The Coordinate-Based Time Bomb

*Failure #3 was about the financial blindspot — assuming bots stay "done." This one is about the architectural reason they don't: most RPA bots navigate by coordinates, not comprehension.*

**The pattern:** Bots built on screen-scraping break whenever the UI changes — which is constantly.

**What happened:** A US-based accounting firm spent 15 months building an RPA bot to automate a segment of its audit workflow. The bot interacted with a tax software application through its graphical user interface, performing data uploads, calculations, and report downloads.

It worked. Until it didn't.

The tax software pushed an interface update. Buttons moved. Fields shifted. The bot — which had been programmed to click specific coordinates on the screen — couldn't find what it was looking for. The entire automation stopped.

This wasn't a one-off. The project team identified that "the biggest challenge on development was incorporating our tax software and getting it to respond," and acknowledged that future upgrades of the tax software could "potentially break the bot." The concern wasn't hypothetical — it was inevitable.

This is the architectural weakness at the heart of traditional RPA: bots follow coordinates, not intent. A typical bot instruction reads: *Click button at coordinates (247, 891). Wait 2 seconds. Type text in field at (156, 423).* It works perfectly — until the button moves one pixel.

**The autopsy:** Modern enterprise software updates constantly. Salesforce releases three times a year. Microsoft 365 updates monthly. SAP follows planned release cycles. For a mid-sized enterprise running 15 interconnected systems, that's **60 potential breaking points per year** for coordinate-based automation.

**The lesson:** If your RPA vendor can't answer "what happens when the ERP interface updates next quarter?" with anything more concrete than "you rebuild the bot" — you're buying a time bomb with a quarterly fuse.

> **Forrester:** Maintenance costs can account for up to 60% of total RPA implementation expenses.

## Failure #5: Nobody Asked the Humans

**The pattern:** The technology works, but the people won't use it — because nobody managed the change.

**What happened:** At the same European telecom firm, RPA's introduction generated immediate resistance from finance staff. Employees withheld process information from bot developers, fearing job losses. Some actively obstructed the automation effort — "informing late, complaining, trying to charm your way out of a Q&A session," as one developer described it. The tension was real enough to slow bot development significantly.

At the accounting firm, adoption was even more telling: despite a working bot, actual usage was low because participation was voluntary and no one championed the tool internally. The firm lacked a dedicated sponsor to drive adoption.

And this is a pattern across the industry. **39% of organisations cite lack of internal expertise as the #1 barrier to RPA success** (Deloitte). Not technology limitations. Not budget constraints. People.

**The autopsy:** Automation is a design problem as much as a technology problem. The question isn't just "can this process be automated?" but "will the humans who interact with this automation accept it, trust it, and maintain it?"

The telecom firm eventually found that once employees were shown what the bots actually did — taking away the repetitive, boring work and freeing them for more challenging tasks — the resistance faded. As one employee put it: "Oh, how nice. I don't need to do this because I can just put it to the robot."

But that turnaround required deliberate communication, change management, and a reframing of what the bots meant for people's roles. None of that happened automatically.

**The lesson:** Your RPA program needs a change management workstream that's budgeted and staffed like the technology workstream. Role redesign, training, communication, and — critically — clear messaging about what happens to the humans whose tasks are being automated.

> **Deloitte:** 37% of RPA failures stem from inadequate change management practices.

## The Self-Diagnosis Checklist

Before your automation program joins the graveyard, run through this honestly.

### Process Health

- [ ] Every process targeted for automation has been documented end-to-end in writing
- [ ] Exception handling rules are defined and documented (not just in someone's head)
- [ ] The process has been stable for at least 6 months with no major redesigns planned
- [ ] You've asked: "Should this process exist at all, or should we redesign it first?"

### Governance

- [ ] Every bot has an assigned owner who is accountable for its uptime
- [ ] A central register exists of all bots, what they do, and when they were last tested
- [ ] There is a defined approval process for new bot deployments
- [ ] Shadow/rogue bot deployments are actively monitored and prevented
- [ ] Documentation standards are enforced (not optional)

### Financial Reality

- [ ] Your business case includes maintenance costs (minimum 20–30% of implementation cost annually)
- [ ] You've budgeted for emergency remediation (UI changes, system updates)
- [ ] Total cost of ownership is calculated over 3 years, not just Year 1
- [ ] You know the difference between license cost and total cost

### Change Management

- [ ] Affected employees have been told what's happening and why
- [ ] Training is planned for new roles and workflows post-automation
- [ ] There is an executive sponsor actively championing the program
- [ ] You've defined what happens to employees whose tasks are automated (upskilled, redeployed — not just "TBD")

### Architecture & Resilience

- [ ] You have a plan for what happens when underlying systems update
- [ ] Bot resilience is tested against simulated UI changes
- [ ] Cross-system workflows have fallback procedures
- [ ] You've evaluated whether your use case needs deterministic RPA or adaptive AI agents

**Score yourself:**
- **16+ checks:** You're building on solid ground
- **10–15 checks:** You have gaps that will hurt at scale
- **Under 10 checks:** You're one system update away from an expensive Monday morning

## The Bigger Picture

The automation graveyard isn't going to stop growing. As enterprises rush to adopt agentic AI on top of already-fragile RPA foundations, the failure rate will accelerate — Gartner predicts **40% of agentic AI projects will be abandoned by 2027**.

The companies that succeed won't be the ones with the most bots. They'll be the ones that treated automation as a design discipline: understanding processes before automating them, governing bots like the production systems they are, budgeting honestly for the long game, and — above all — remembering that the humans in the loop aren't obstacles to automation. They're the reason it exists.

The graveyard has room. The question is whether you'll learn from the headstones or add your own.
